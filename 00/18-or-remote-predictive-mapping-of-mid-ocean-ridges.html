Overview

Remote predictive mapping of mid-ocean ridges

Meike Klischies, geoscientist

What does the graphic show?:

Geological map of the Rodriguez Triple Junction in the central Indian Ocean with terrain texture shaded, ship-based bathymetry in the background.

Personal perspective:

Geological maps are a basic tool for geoscientists and of use for a broad variety of disciplines, including biology (e.g., habitat mapping) and geochemistry (e.g., in choosing sampling locations). The presented map visualizes the construction of the upper ocean crust and the setting of hydrothermal venting and associated seafloor massive sulphides. It allows to reconstruct area-age relationships and the dynamics of magmatic and tectonic processes along the mid-ocean ridges that meet at the Rodriguez Triple Junction.

How was this visualization created?:

We use remote sensing data, especially ship-based bathymetry, to produce a geological map. The data is compiled and the mapping is conducted manually in a geographic information system (here, ArcGIS), which is also used to produce the presented map layout. Geological mapping from remote sensing data is common practice in hardly-accessible areas on land (e.g., swamps, ice-covered areas) and on other planetary objects. Here, we adapt this remote predictive mapping approach to the seafloor environment, where sampling and ground truthing are restricted to individual, isolated locations.
